# Loom Beta Requirements

Captures the concrete decisions behind Loom's evidentiary,
hardware, performance, and collaboration defaults for the beta
release. Defaults assume an Illinois-based user (Cook County and
federal venues) because that is where the first production cases
will be filed. Deployments outside Illinois may need to override
specific values; each section flags what is jurisdiction-sensitive.

Closes #48.

## Jurisdiction and evidentiary standards

### Target courts

- **Primary**: Circuit Court of Cook County, Illinois (civil and
  criminal divisions) and Illinois Appellate Court, First
  District.
- **Secondary**: United States District Court for the Northern
  District of Illinois (federal § 1983 civil-rights actions).
- **Statewide**: other Illinois Circuit Courts that follow the
  Illinois Rules of Evidence (IRE) as adopted 2010 and amended
  2018+.

Loom's export format must be admissible under both the IRE and
the Federal Rules of Evidence (FRE). The two overlap on
authentication (IRE/FRE 901) and the business-records exception
to hearsay (IRE/FRE 803(6), 902(11)), so the export bundle is
designed to satisfy the more demanding federal path — FRE 902(13)
and 902(14) self-authentication by hash certification — which
also satisfies the Illinois path.

### Rules of evidence applied

- **Authentication — FRE 901(a) / IRE 901(a)**: proponent must
  produce "evidence sufficient to support a finding that the item
  is what the proponent claims it is." Loom satisfies this by
  recording, at ingest, the SHA-256 of every original file, the
  ingesting user, the device/browser, the timestamp, and an
  append-only chain-of-custody entry.
- **Self-authentication of electronic records — FRE 902(13)**:
  records generated by an electronic process or system that
  produces accurate results, accompanied by a qualified-person
  certification meeting FRE 902(11)/(12). The export bundle
  includes a machine-readable certification block suitable for
  attachment to a § 902 certificate.
- **Self-authentication of copied data — FRE 902(14)**: data
  copied from an electronic device, authenticated by digital
  identification (a hash), with qualified-person certification.
  Loom's export ships the SHA-256 of every asset alongside the
  asset itself and the re-hash on open.
- **Business records exception — IRE 803(6), 902(11)**: Illinois
  has not adopted 902(13)/(14) verbatim, so the exported
  custodian certification is also drafted in the form expected by
  IRE 902(11): statement that records were made at or near the
  time of the event, by someone with knowledge, kept in the
  regular course of activity, and that making such records was a
  regular practice.

### Hashing algorithm

- **SHA-256** is the default and only algorithm for original-file
  integrity, derivative integrity, and bundle manifests. NIST
  recommends SHA-256 for digital evidence verification; federal
  and Illinois courts accept it as the de facto standard under
  FRE 902(14).
- MD5 and SHA-1 are **not** offered. They are deprecated for
  evidentiary hashing and would weaken a 902(14) certification.
- The bundle manifest itself is hashed (SHA-256 of the canonical
  JSON) and the manifest hash is the single value the custodian
  attests to on a printed certificate.

### Chain-of-custody format

The append-only `chain_of_custody_entries` table captures, per
event:

- UTC timestamp with millisecond precision
- actor (user id + email at time of action)
- action (`ingested`, `verified`, `rehashed`, `proxy_generated`,
  `annotated`, `redaction_created`, `exported`, `imported`,
  `data_dir_relocated`, `deleted`)
- SHA-256 before and after where relevant
- client metadata (user agent, source IP hash, device
  fingerprint)
- signed statement text (argon2-hash-signed, not asymmetric yet —
  see "Deferred" below)

Export bundles include the full chain of custody for each asset
rendered as both:

- a line-per-event CSV (paralegal-readable, drops into exhibit
  preparation); and
- a JSON-LD document (machine-verifiable, carries the hash
  lineage).

This matches the Illinois Officer-Worn Body Camera Act's custody
documentation expectations (who downloaded, when, hash) and the
Chicago Police Department's body-worn camera special order
S03-14.

### PDF vs native format preferences

- **Native originals are never converted.** The original file
  lands in WORM storage byte-for-byte; the court receives the
  original plus its hash.
- **Derivatives** (proxies, transcripts, redacted videos) are
  rendered in standard formats courts accept: MP4/H.264 for
  video, PDF/A-2b for any printed report, and UTF-8 plain text or
  SRT for transcripts.
- **Court reports** are exported as PDF/A-2b (archival PDF). Not
  regular PDF — PDF/A is the format the Administrative Office of
  the U.S. Courts recommends and that many Illinois electronic
  filing systems require.
- **Timelines** export as both a human-readable PDF/A-2b report
  and a machine-readable JSON-LD document, so the timeline can be
  re-ingested or re-verified by any conforming verifier.

### Notary / authentication requirements

- **Baseline**: the export bundle is self-authenticating under
  FRE 902(13)/(14). A custodian certificate signed by the case
  owner (under penalty of perjury via 28 U.S.C. § 1746 or
  Illinois equivalent 735 ILCS 5/1-109) is included in every
  bundle. No notary is required for the base admissibility path.
- **When a notary is needed** (rare, but some state courts still
  require it for hostile evidentiary challenges): the bundle
  includes a printable signature block formatted for notarization
  of the custodian statement. This is a paper step — Loom does
  not sign anything with a digital notary service.
- **Not in scope for beta**: trusted-timestamping (RFC 3161),
  blockchain anchoring, and third-party attestation services.
  These are tracked as follow-ups because they add external
  dependencies that are difficult to maintain on a field laptop.

## Hardware targets

### Typical legal-observer laptop (2026)

- **CPU**: Intel Core i5 12th gen or Apple M1 class or AMD
  Ryzen 5 5000-series.
- **RAM**: 16 GB.
- **Storage**: 512 GB NVMe SSD.
- **OS**: Windows 11, macOS 13+, or Ubuntu LTS 22.04/24.04.
- **Screen**: 13–16 inch, 1920×1080 or higher.

Field laptops are personal devices in most cases. Desktop Lite
is the supported profile for this class of hardware.

### Minimum specs for Desktop Lite

- **CPU**: 64-bit x86-64 or arm64, 2 cores, AVX2 (required by
  some AI-extra binaries).
- **RAM**: 8 GB (AI extras disabled) / 16 GB (AI extras enabled).
- **Storage**: 20 GB free on the application drive + a data
  directory with at least 2× the expected case footage size
  free. See Dataset targets below.
- **OS**: Windows 10 21H2+, macOS 11+, Ubuntu 22.04+.
- **WebView**: WebView2 on Windows (auto-installed), system
  WebKit on macOS, `libwebkit2gtk-4.1` on Linux.

If AI extras (transcription, scene detection, OCR) are enabled
below the recommended RAM, they degrade to sequential processing
with longer wall-clock time rather than failing.

### External drives and NAS

- **External drives (USB 3 / Thunderbolt)**: assumed. The first-
  run flow lets users pick any writable path, including an
  external mount. See #47 for the external-storage UX work.
- **NAS / SMB / NFS**: supported as a data directory target with
  caveats: the WORM read-only flag is set via OS metadata, which
  some network filesystems silently drop. Loom will surface a
  warning at first run if the picked path's filesystem does not
  support the chmod-readonly flag, and the ingest pipeline
  re-verifies the read-only bit after every write.
- **Cloud drives (iCloud, OneDrive, Google Drive)**: **not
  supported as a data directory**. They rewrite files during
  sync, which breaks hash integrity. First-run detects these
  paths and refuses to pick them.

## Dataset and performance targets

### Max dataset size per case

- **Target**: up to **10 TB** per case, mixed video + photo +
  audio + document.
- **Equivalent duration**: approximately 2,000 hours of 1080p H.264
  footage at ~5 Mbps, or 1,000 hours of 4K at ~10 Mbps.
- **Asset count**: up to **100,000 assets** per case before
  timeline-filter latency targets start to degrade.

The server profile scales past these limits via horizontal
worker scaling. Desktop Lite is designed for a case on one
machine; users with >10 TB should run the server deploy or split
into linked sub-cases.

### Ingest throughput

On the target hardware (16 GB RAM, NVMe SSD), with AI extras
**disabled**:

- Hash + metadata + WORM-write: **≥ 5 hours of 1080p H.264 per
  hour of wall-clock** on a single-worker laptop.
- Proxy generation (H.264 → 720p H.264, 2 Mbps): **≥ 2 hours of
  1080p per hour of wall-clock**.

With AI extras **enabled** on the same hardware:

- Transcription (faster-whisper small): **≥ 3× realtime**.
- OCR (pytesseract, page-level): **≥ 30 pages per minute**.
- Scene detection: **≥ 10× realtime**.

These are target floors, not SLAs. They are monitored by the
existing Prometheus metrics (`loom_activity_duration_seconds`)
and regressions are flagged in CI.

### Timeline-filter latency

On a case with 100,000 events:

- **p95 filter-by-tag or time-range**: ≤ 500 ms.
- **p95 full-text search across transcripts + OCR + annotations**:
  ≤ 1,500 ms.

These targets assume the case's database (SQLite on Desktop Lite,
Postgres on server) has run its routine maintenance (`VACUUM` /
`ANALYZE`). They are measured with the existing timeline filter
benchmark harness.

## Collaboration scope

### RBAC confirmation

The existing model is kept for beta:

- **System roles**: `admin`, `analyst`, `viewer`.
- **Case roles**: `owner`, `editor`, `viewer`.
- **Organization roles**: `admin`, `member`.
- **`case_memberships`** joins users to cases with their case
  role, independent of their system role.

This matches the described workflow: one case owner drives
synthesis; editors contribute annotations and timeline events;
viewers can read and export but cannot modify. Admins exist for
organizational administration, not case content.

### Expected team sizes

- **Typical case**: 2–5 collaborators (owner, 1–2 editors,
  1–2 viewers for counsel review).
- **Large coordinated action**: up to **20** collaborators
  across multiple organizations, with most as viewers.

Performance targets in the previous section are measured at the
upper bound of typical team sizes. The server profile is the
supported deployment for teams above 10.

### Cross-organization sharing

- **In scope for beta**: export a signed court-bundle from one
  deploy and import into another. The bundle format carries
  everything a second deploy needs to verify hashes, rebuild the
  timeline, and re-render the report.
- **Out of scope for beta**: live cross-organization sharing.
  Shared evidence links across organizations, real-time
  collaborative editing across deploys, and federated identity
  are deferred (tracked by #42 for multi-device sync and by a
  follow-up federation issue).

## Privacy and compliance

### Applicable privacy laws (Illinois primary)

- **Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA)** —
  740 ILCS 14/: requires written consent before collecting a
  biometric identifier. "Biometric identifier" includes a
  voiceprint, face-geometry scan, iris scan, fingerprint, or
  hand-geometry scan; photographs and raw video are explicitly
  excluded from the definition. Loom's core principles already
  prohibit face recognition, suspicion scoring, and automated
  identity resolution — these are structural guarantees, not
  settings. To stay BIPA-safe on the AI side:
  - **Transcription** is text-only; faster-whisper does not
    persist speaker embeddings.
  - **Speaker diarization** via pyannote is **off by default**
    on Desktop Lite and must be explicitly enabled on the server
    profile; the embedding vectors are **not** persisted, only
    the diarized segment boundaries and labels (`Speaker 1`,
    `Speaker 2`). No voiceprint is ever retained.
  - **Face blurring** (a redaction feature) does not require
    identifying the face — it runs a generic detector and
    produces a blur mask. Detection coordinates are stored; face
    geometry vectors are not.
- **Illinois Eavesdropping Act** — 720 ILCS 5/14-2: recording
  a law-enforcement officer performing duties in public is
  legal. Recording a conversation in which a participant has a
  reasonable expectation of privacy requires all-party consent.
  Loom does not itself record; it ingests recordings the user
  made or obtained. The user is responsible for the legality of
  the recording; Loom's annotations schema includes a `consent`
  field on every source asset so the case file can carry consent
  metadata forward to export.
- **Federal**: 18 U.S.C. § 2511 (Wiretap Act) — one-party
  consent at the federal level; Illinois's stricter
  all-party-consent rule governs within Illinois.
- **FOIA / Illinois FOIA (5 ILCS 140/)**: relevant when the
  source material came from a public-records request. Loom
  preserves the original request metadata as an ingest-source
  annotation so provenance traces back to the FOIA response.

Deployments outside Illinois should consult local privacy law.
States with similar biometric-privacy statutes (TX, WA) receive
the same treatment as Illinois; states without one still
benefit from the BIPA-aligned defaults.

### Retention and deletion

**Defaults** (user-configurable per case):

- **Original assets**: **retained until the case owner
  explicitly marks the case as closed and initiates purge.** No
  automatic deletion. Chain-of-custody rationale: a purge before
  final disposition would break admissibility.
- **Derivatives** (proxies, transcripts, OCR, redactions):
  retained for the life of the original. They can be regenerated
  from the original.
- **Audit log and chain-of-custody**: **append-only, indefinite
  retention**. Enforced at the database level (ORM listeners +
  Postgres triggers on the server profile; application-level
  enforcement on SQLite).
- **Revoked JWTs**: retained for the max token lifetime (7 days)
  plus a 24-hour grace period, then purged.

**Alignment with Illinois body-camera retention** (50 ILCS
706/10): body-cam footage is retained for 90 days minimum, 2
years if flagged, indefinitely if part of a proceeding. Loom's
defaults are strictly more conservative than this baseline —
nothing is auto-deleted until the case owner purges.

**Case purge**: a destructive operation. It requires:

1. case-owner role,
2. a typed confirmation of the case title, and
3. a reason recorded in the audit log.

The operation deletes originals, derivatives, and annotations
but **keeps the audit-log tombstone** (user, timestamp, case
title, asset hashes at purge time) for as long as the system
runs. Purge is irreversible.

### Export deletion

Exported bundles leave Loom's custody at the point of export.
Downstream handling (court filing, discovery transmission,
archival) is the attorney's responsibility. Every export is
logged in `export_bundles` and the audit log, with the bundle
hash, so the case owner can attest to what was handed out.

## Deferred / out of scope for beta

Tracked separately so they don't expand the beta surface:

- **Trusted timestamping (RFC 3161)** and blockchain anchoring —
  would strengthen non-repudiation but add external dependencies.
- **Asymmetric signing of chain-of-custody entries** (Ed25519
  per-install keys) — current signing is HMAC with the install's
  `LOOM_STORAGE_SIGNING_SECRET`. Asymmetric signing is cleaner
  for multi-party verification but requires a key-management
  story.
- **Multi-device sync** — #42.
- **Federated identity / SSO** across deploys — follow-up issue.
- **Court-specific export profiles** (Cook County ECF, PACER,
  state-specific e-filing) — beta exports the generic bundle and
  PDF/A-2b report; court-specific profiles are added on demand.
